Bangladesh

Begum Rokeya

Begum Rokeya (1880-1932) was a pioneering social reformer, educationist, and feminist in colonial Bengal whose advocacy for women’s rights laid the foundation for modern feminist thought in Bangladesh. Rokeya championed female education at a time when societal norms severely restricted women’s access to learning, founding the Sakhawat Memorial Girls’ School in Kolkata in 1911. Her writings, including Sultana’s Dream (1905), employed both imaginative and reformist strategies to challenge patriarchal norms, critique gender inequality, and envision a society where women could participate fully in public, intellectual, and political life. Rokeya’s emphasis on education as a tool for emancipation, combined with her critique of social and religious restrictions on women, established her as a transformative figure whose work continues to inspire gender equity movements in Bangladesh.

"The era is over when men would trample on us and still have us licking their boots."

— Begum Rokeya

Shireen Huq

Shireen Huq is a contemporary Bangladeshi feminist, scholar, and activist whose work has significantly advanced the discourse on women’s rights and gender-based violence in Bangladesh. As a researcher and co-founder of Naripokkho, a prominent women’s rights organization, Huq has focused on creating legal, social, and policy frameworks to protect women from structural and domestic violence, emphasizing participatory and evidence-based approaches. Her scholarship and activism have highlighted the intersection of gender, development, and human rights, advocating for legislative reform, gender-sensitive policies, and societal awareness. Through her tireless efforts, Huq has contributed to greater recognition of women’s autonomy, empowerment, and protection under Bangladeshi law, influencing both grassroots movements and national policy.


India

Ela Bhatt

Ela Bhatt (1933-2022) was a pioneering Indian social activist and feminist whose work profoundly influenced women’s economic empowerment in South Asia. As the founder of the Self-Employed Women’s Association (SEWA) in 1972, Bhatt organized women working in the informal sector—such as street vendors, artisans, and home-based workers—into a collective that championed labor rights, fair wages, and social security. Her feminist vision combined economic justice with gender equity, emphasizing the dignity and autonomy of marginalized women. Bhatt’s work has had a lasting impact, providing millions of women with platforms for self-reliance, collective bargaining, and leadership, while inspiring feminist labor movements across the region.

"Women are the leaders in change and without their participation poverty can never be removed."

— Ela Bhatt

Trinetra Haldar Gummaraju

Dr. Trinetra Haldar Gummaraju is an Indian trans rights activist, feminist, and physician whose advocacy bridges gender, sexuality, and health equity. Gummaraju’s work focuses on amplifying marginalized voices, combating social stigma, and promoting legal and social recognition for transgender individuals, particularly in South Asia. As a public educator and writer, she integrates feminist principles with intersectional advocacy, addressing the unique vulnerabilities of trans women and gender-diverse communities. Through activism, storytelling, and scholarship, Gummaraju has contributed to expanding the feminist framework in India to be more inclusive, intersectional, and attuned to contemporary struggles around identity, healthcare, and social justice.

"Today, it's about making a difference and taking up space where it matters."

— Trinetra Gummaraju

Shrishti Bakshi

Srishti Bakshi is an Indian gender rights activist and social entrepreneur renowned for her innovative approach to addressing gender-based violence and promoting women's empowerment. In 2017, Bakshi embarked on an ambitious 3,800-kilometer on-foot journey across India—from Kanyakumari to Kashmir—under the initiative CrossBow Miles. This endeavor aimed to raise awareness about women's safety and to empower women through digital and financial literacy. Bakshi's journey culminated in the documentary WOMB: Women of My Billion, which documents her experiences and the stories of women she encountered during her travels.

Pakistan

Qandeel Baloch

Qandeel Baloch (1990-2016) was a Pakistani social media personality and feminist icon whose life and tragic death sparked national and international debates on gender, honor, and women's autonomy in Pakistan. Known for challenging conservative social norms through bold self-expression and commentary on women’s rights, Baloch used platforms like Facebook and Instagram to confront patriarchal restrictions, question societal expectations, and amplify feminist discourse in a highly restrictive environment. Her activism—though informal and often controversial—highlighted the dangers faced by women who defy traditional norms, exposing the culture of honor-based violence, misogyny, and victim-blaming. Baloch’s life and untimely murder galvanized conversations on digital freedom, women's empowerment, and the urgent need for legal and social protections for women in Pakistan.

"I believe I am a modern day feminist. I believe in equality. I am just a woman with free thoughts free mindset and I love the way I am"

— Qandeel Baloch

Sharmeen Obaid-Chinoy

Sharmeen Obaid-Chinoy is a Pakistani journalist, documentary filmmaker, and activist whose work has significantly impacted feminist discourse and social reform in South Asia. As a two-time Academy Award winner, Obaid-Chinoy uses visual storytelling to illuminate issues of gender-based violence, honor killings, and systemic discrimination against women. Her documentaries, such as Saving Face (2012) and A Girl in the River: The Price of Forgiveness (2015), combine investigative rigor with empathetic narrative to raise awareness, influence public policy, and mobilize legal reforms in Pakistan. Obaid-Chinoy’s work bridges art and activism, making her a crucial figure in both documenting and advocating for women’s rights.
