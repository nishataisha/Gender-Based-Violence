Weak Legal Frameworks

In South Asia, legal frameworks addressing gender-based violence (GBV) are often informed by patriarchal cultural norms that tether a woman’s value to her perceived modesty and behavior. This socio-legal construct positions women as bearers of familial honor, thereby translating notions of modesty into legislative language that centers protection over prevention through safeguarding “virtue” within the community (Nainar, 2023).

Although India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh have all enacted laws aimed at curbing Gender-Based Violence (GBV), they remain largely ineffective due to the vague terminology and their prioritization of protecting a woman’s honor, modesty, and social standing over justice. This is not uncalled for since the gender imbalance in legislative bodies further exacerbates these issues (Naraharisetty, 2022).

According to UN Women, only 15–20% of political positions in India and Pakistan, and 20.9% in Bangladesh, are held by women, limiting female perspectives in lawmaking and policymaking (UN Women, 2023; UN Women Data Hub, 2024). In the absence of gender-inclusive governance, laws designed to protect women are frequently authored and interpreted through a male-centric lens, resulting in provisions that, while well-intentioned, often serve to shield perpetrators and perpetuate systemic inequality.

A prominent example is Section 354 in the Indian Penal Code (IPC) pre-amendment 2013, which describes sexual assault as the intent to outrage a woman’s modesty, providing loopholes for the offender to argue that their victim does not hold a sense of modesty due to their age, caste, or occupation. This was seen in State of Punjab v. Major Singh (AIR 1967 SC 63), where a sexual offender violated a 7.5-month-old victim, with the main defense for the perpetrator being that ‘modesty’ was inapplicable due to her age.

India has seen some reform in its laws in response to public outrage over sexual violence cases over the years. While these amendments appear to be protective, their language often reinforces patriarchal norms by centering female “dignity” rather than autonomy. Section 509 of the IPC criminalizes acts that insult the “modesty” of a woman, and Section 228-A can result in dehumanization, turning victims into symbols of shame and sorrow rather than individuals with agency, especially in cases where the victim has passed away and cannot reclaim her narrative.

Similarly, Pakistan’s Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act (PECA) of 2016 is hindered by ambiguous wording and excessive emphasis on “morality,” an undefined ideology, even when mentioned. While theoretically it provides mechanisms to combat cyber harassment, its enforcement is marred by a lack of police training in gender sensitivity and digital literacy (Naseer & Ashraf, 2021), due to which victims are often silenced by shame, fear, or intimidation from law enforcement itself.

This incompetence in tackling gender-based violence is seen in the 2021 case of Marya Zulfiqar, where the Pakistan police force was informed about her being threatened and accused of holding her at gunpoint, after which the police made no effort to give her protection or bring the accused in for questioning.

Legal amendments have also been seen in Pakistan, one being the Protection of Women Criminal Laws (Amendment) Act 2006, which nominally expanded protections but did so through the lens of controlling women’s sexual behavior, rather than empowering victims or addressing systemic inequalities.

Whereas in Bangladesh, steps are being taken to commence a law that would benefit victims of cybercrimes such as deep fakes, impersonation, and identity theft. The Cyber Security Act, 2023 (Act No. 39 of 2023) states that anyone who intentionally uses a computer network or digital device to assume another person's identity or present their personal information with intent to deceive or defraud will face a penalty of up to 5 years in prison, or a fine of 0.5 million Taka (approximately 4,000 USD), or both (সাইবার নিরাপত্তা আইন, ২০২৩, অধ্যায় ৬). In addition to the long legal processes, the Cybersecurity Act of 2023 is yet to be finalised, which adds further hurdles to those seeking justice.

Bangladesh witnessed its first case of celebrity deepfake in 2023 when a sexually explicit video went viral across social media platforms, with many falsely claiming that it featured the popular actress Tanjin Tisha. Soon after, an online fact-checking and media research organization by the name Dismislab confirmed that the video was a fabrication. The footage belonged to a pornographic website and had no connection to the actress. The digitally manipulated video using deepfake technology produced a pornographic video that had Tanjin Tisha’s face in another woman’s body (Hossaion, 2024). This incident exposed the dangers and vulnerabilities against targeted gender-based cyber violence in Bangladesh.

A similar case occurred with another actress, Sabnam Faria, who publicly addressed the misuse of another person’s pictures digitally altered and passed around as hers online. In a candid Facebook post on March 31st 2024, she wrote in a resistant sarcastic yet defiant tone: “For quite some time now, someone has been editing this person’s pictures and putting my face on them, and posting them on Facebook. I don’t know who these people are or what they want. Do you really have such a strong desire to see me in shorts? When I travel abroad, I already post photos wearing shorts! If those photos aren’t revealing enough and that disappoints you, then I apologize! But now one of your 'big brothers' has gone and edited her photo with my face again!!!” (Faria, 2024). Unfortunately her slamming remarks weren’t enough to stop the culprits. Just a month later, on April 28th, she had to address another case of impersonation of herself with an altered photo of another person on her social media (Faria, 2024).

Revenge Porn

One of the most prevalent forms of Digital Gender-Based Violence is Revenge Porn, defined by Merriam-Webster’s dictionary as “sexually explicit images of a person posted online without that person’s consent especially as a form of revenge or harassment” (Merriam-Webster Dictionary, 2015). This form of digital abuse is used to weaponize and dehumanize women, signaling their supposed sexual availability on the internet.

The most common form of revenge porn in South-Asia is the perpetrator impersonating the victim online, and creating fake profiles that share sexualized imagery under the guise of the victim’s identity. It not only violates a woman’s privacy but also directly threatens her safety, izzat (reputation), and her life in society, where a woman’s perceived “honor” is closely tied to her family’s dignity; being falsely portrayed as promiscuous harms her and her family’s social identity.

This phenomenon is seen through crimes like the circulation of revenge porn of an ex-girlfriend in response to failed extortion by Bhubaneswar in India (Mohapatra, 2024); blackmailing Sanam Maher by an ex-boyfriend in Pakistan (Yousafzai, 2022); spreading intimate pictures by the fake ID Shimanto Chowdhury in Bangladesh, show the victim’s invasion of privacy by an entrusted ex-romantic partner. In all of these cases, the perpetrator coerces the victim to comply with their demands or face becoming a social pariah.

However, revenge porn can also stem from ostracism of individuals as seen by the first case of revenge porn in India in 2001, which shows that these crimes are not only related to unsuccessful romantic relationships but also social ones. A 16-year-old student, who was bailed for making a pornographic website of his classmates and teachers, who socially ostracised him through provoking and poking fun at him. The perpetrator was brought in for questioning and was eventually bailed and rehabilitated, since the court deemed this case as an example of ‘tech-graffiti’ similar to bathroom graffiti, as a concern for his future and well-being.

As internet accessibility continues to expand, the number of reported cases of digital gender-based violence in South Asia increased by 5% — reaching 50,035 cases between 2020 and 2021, highlighting the growing scope of this issue. Despite this rise, accessible and reliable statistical data remain scarce, reflecting a broader governmental lack of initiative and concern.

Social Stigma & Victim Blaming

Izzat, which directly translates to honor or respect, is a deeply rooted cultural concept in South Asia that represents social standing (add more here, Aisha). This concept not only applies to individuals but also to families aiming to maintain or elevate their societal standing, especially for women, who are seen as the custodians of honor through ideas of dignity, chastity, and moral conduct. This linkage is reinforced through cultural norms, religious interpretations, and social practices that prescribe strict codes of conduct for women, thereby making female purity and dignity central to collective honor.

As a result, women’s bodies and choices become sites of control and surveillance in the name of protecting family or community prestige. This regulation of women can be broadly categorized into two interrelated forms: familial regulation and self-regulation. Familial regulation usually encompasses external control by family members, manifesting through restrictions online and offline on a woman’s social interactions, mobility, and activity. In extreme cases, a woman may be subjected to an “honor killing” or “karo-kari,” rooted in patriarchal traditions where women are murdered by family members for supposed dishonor, through disobedience. Some notable reasons include refusing an arranged marriage, adopting Western lifestyles, or seeking independence.

The case of Qandeel Baloch, a Pakistani social media personality whose online presence challenged traditional norms, resulted in moral policing and eventually her tragic murder on July 12, 2016, by her brother (CITATION). Perpetrators such as Muhammad Waseem were later pardoned by her mother under Section 311 of Pakistan’s Penal Code, which allows a victim’s family to forgive the assailant—highlighting how “honor” is used to undermine accountability.

Consequences of Stigma

Despite the severity of these societal norms, religious communities often frame these acts as “domestic issues,” promoting backward and violently patriarchal ideals. Yet Ali & Zafar (2020) argue that these crimes are driven by social customs—not Islam. Family honor, community pressure, and even admiration for the assailant’s “bravery” combine to maintain these extremist practices.

As for comparatively “trivial” consequences, victims may face societal restrictions and are often blamed for the crime and its consequences due to their actions—whether being outspoken, dressed “immodestly,” or walking without a male chaperone (Rawat, 2017). Hence, women facing online abuse often refrain from reporting incidents out of fear of causing family dishonor, withdrawing from digital platforms altogether to protect their families from social scrutiny (Digital Rights Foundation, 2020; Internet Democracy Project, 2019).

In extreme cases, the law enforcement’s failure to take swift and exemplary action against online harm adds to the victim’s fear. After a female student from Dhaka University was harassed for her clothing, the perpetrator was released on bail within hours under pressure from a religious political party. The victim’s personal details were shared online, putting her family in immediate danger. She said, “I worry whether the state will provide enough security for me. I saw the accused being greeted with flowers, while I feel I have to cover my face if I leave the house. What would be the point in continuing this case?” (The Daily Star, 2025).

Psychological Impact and Self‐Blame

In the circumstance of losing control over their narrative, social ideologies like victim blaming cause extreme psychological distress through societal alienation and deep‐rooted guilt—resulting in the individual blaming themselves instead of the perpetrators. This can manifest as anxiety, depression, and PTSD, contributing to a disrupted self‐concept clarity (Ali, 2025; Halder & Jaishankar, 2013).

Digital platforms worsen this dynamic by enabling repeated exposure and secondary victimization, reinforcing misogynistic stereotypes and social stigmas (Tonny & Nawaz, 2025; Naseet & Ashrad, 2022). This vicious cycle normalizes harassment within social and online settings, leaving women emotionally burdened by constant threats to their safety (Chinchu, 2024; Bessant & Watts, 2021).

A longitudinal study of 100 sexually abused adolescent girls found that feelings of shame predicted PTSD symptoms six months later, while self‐blame strongly correlated with severe depression. Avoidance coping (suppressing emotions) and ongoing depression contributed to suicidal thoughts—over half of the participants reported suicidal ideation. Six months later, nearly half had PTSD symptoms, over a third faced depression, and 30% reported suicidal thoughts (Alix et al., 2020).

Men’s Rights & Anti‐Feminist Movements

The culture of silence and blame is further weaponized in digital spaces, where the rhetoric of the Men’s Rights Movement (MRM) in India reframes victims of sexual assault as manipulative and perpetrators as victims of a “biased system”; referring to several sections of the Indian constitution as “made for the protection of women” despite the inherently patriarchal structure of Indian society. These criticisms of the law and its enforcers are especially magnified in cases of custody, alimony, divorce, and domestic violence (Desai, 2023). What began as a purported defense of men’s legal rights has increasingly devolved into a justification for misogyny, the dismissal of gender-based violence, and the vilification of feminism.

The evolution of Web 2.0 saw the growth of platforms like Reddit, YouTube, and Instagram, giving a platform for open communication to the public and allowing MRM ideologies to take root in anonymous online spaces (Desai, 2023). While the manosphere rhetoric originated in the West, in South Asia—particularly India—it is shaped by socio-religious conservatism and linguistic diversity. A notable concept they use is the idea of the Adarsh Bhartiya Nari, which translates to the Ideal Indian Woman, who is a submissive female archetype, devoutly imbues Indian values to frame feminism as a threat to national culture and morality (Priya & Sigamany, 2025).

Explicit vs. Implicit Misogyny

Online misogyny in these regions often manifests in two forms: explicit and implicit. Waseem et al. (2017) classify explicit misogyny as personal insults to the victim using sexually derogatory targeted speech. An example of explicit misogyny is the online harassment Rhea Chakraborty faced following Sushant Singh Rajput’s 2020 suicide. She was branded a “gold digger” and blamed for his death—despite the lack of evidence. In a study examining 2,128 tweets with relevant hashtags to the case, the majority contained misogynistic remarks toward Chakraborty (Nanditha et al., 2023).

On the other hand, implicit misogyny operates through sarcasm, cynicism, and subtle accusations (Waseem et al., 2017). Influencer Apoorva Mukhija, who had a viral altercation on a talent show called “India’s Got Latent,” faced rape, acid attack, and death threats. On April 8, 2025, she posted on Instagram titled “and that’s not even 1%,” sharing uncensored comments she received after the incident. Although her supporters offered positivity, many comments resorted to sarcasm, speculation, and insinuation—implying she only revealed the threats to gain sympathy and downplay the severity of the harassment.

Weaponizing Misinformation

The deliberate exaggeration and weaponization of misinformation by red-pill influencers cultivates mistrust toward women and dilutes public empathy toward victims. By selectively amplifying the occurrence of false accusations, these individuals fuel a broader ecosystem of doubt and misogyny. Their aim is not factual discourse but rather disregarding structural gender-based violence and reframing male discomfort with shifting gender norms as victimhood.

Bollywood & Regional Cinema

Cinema in the Indian subcontinent holds deep cultural significance, particularly for the middle class, influencing values, identity, and gender norms. A key example is the ideal of the Adarsh Bhartiya Nari (Ideal Indian Woman), heavily promoted by early Bollywood films. These narratives often placed the burden of preserving purity — and by extension, the nation’s honor — on women. This is clearly seen in the blockbuster movie Dilwale Dulhania Le Jayenge. Such tropes reduce women to submissive “damsels in distress,” told through the lens of a dominant male hero.

Even today, films like Kabir Singh (2019) and Animal (2023), directed by Sandeep Reddy Vanga, portray misogynistic and hyper‐masculine characters whose abusive behavior is glorified (Khan & Taylor, 2023). Despite some progress toward more nuanced female roles, mainstream Bollywood continues to objectify women through “item songs” and female characters whose worth is tied to male redemption (Shewade, 2023).

Pakistani & Bangladeshi Media

In Pakistan, mainstream dramas often trivialize domestic abuse, pitting women against each other, portraying them as “evil” or “promiscuous,” and glorifying patriarchal values (Khan, 2020). Ahmar, director of Uks (media advocacy in Pakistan), states: “99.9% of dramas on Pakistani television channels are ‘sexist, repressive, and regressive’” (Sarfaraz, 2020). Even when some dramas push back, the broader industry continues to sideline strong female characters and normalize misogyny.

Bangladeshi cinema similarly often reduces women to caregivers, victims, or objects of desire. Films such as Valobashlei Ghor Bandha Jay Na and Poramon 2 depict female protagonists subjected to violence or exploitation. The “dumb woman” trope dehumanizes women by portraying them as naive or weak (Chacchu, 2006; Apu Biswas’s character). Such portrayals normalize violence and perpetuate misogynistic narratives.

Digital Feminism & Counter‐Activism

Collective activism and feminist identification have proven effective at redirecting shame away from victims and toward perpetrators, providing community support (Dey, 2019). Digital feminism in South Asia leverages platforms like Twitter, Instagram, and YouTube to amplify women’s voices, share resources, and build solidarity.

Hashtags like #MeTooIndia and #DigitalIzzat have mobilized thousands to share personal testimonies, demand legal reform, and hold perpetrators accountable. Feminist collectives host online workshops about digital safety, self‐defense, and reporting procedures. They also partner with legal aid organizations to provide pro bono counsel to survivors of DGBV.

In Bangladesh, groups such as Protichi and Priti+ curate fact‐checked resources about cyber harassment and share step‐by‐step guides for filing police complaints under the new Cybersecurity Act (2023). They run “#NoMoreSilence” campaigns, urging young women to refuse self‐censorship and speak openly about their experiences.

In Pakistan, War Against Rape and CyberTehrik use Twitter threads, live webinars, and digital petitions to pressure lawmakers for stricter enforcement of PECA and gender‐sensitive police training. They also train grassroots volunteers in rural areas to help survivors document online abuse and navigate the legal system.

Key Strategies

  • Hashtag Activism: Mobilize testimony-sharing via #MeTooSouthAsia, #DigitalIzzat, #NotYourIzzat.
  • Digital Self‐Defense Training: Partner with tech collectives to teach encryption, anonymized reporting, and evidence preservation.
  • Online Legal Clinics: Create Zoom/Google Meet webinars where pro bono lawyers walk survivors through filing complaints and getting protection orders.
  • Collaborations with Influencers: Work with high‐reach feminist influencers to run micro‐campaigns on YouTube and Instagram Reels debunking rape myths and calling out digital abusers.
  • Media Monitoring: Track mainstream media and film/television for harmful stereotypes, then publish counter‐ analyses in digital newsletters and collaborative Google Docs (open to civil society groups).

Conclusion

Digital feminism and counter‐activism are critical to dismantling patriarchal narratives online. By building supportive networks, offering legal resources, and challenging misogynistic media, South Asian feminist activists have begun to shift public discourse toward accountability, empathy, and structural change. The journey is far from over, but these grassroots efforts demonstrate that collective digital resistance can reshape entire cultural paradigms.
