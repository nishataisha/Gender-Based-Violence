Gender-Based Violence Against Women

This website examines the prevalence and manifestations of Digital Gender-Based Violence (DGBV) against women in South Asia, with a focus on India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh. While the full scope of women's experiences cannot be encapsulated within a single work, this website examines selected dimensions of the struggles—from pervasive societal pressures to systemic legislative shortcomings. By situating these issues within broader socio-cultural and legal contexts, it highlights the persistent failure of instituions at every level to ensure justice and protections for women.


Before beginning the content, it is important to provide an overview of the countries under discussion. The maps below present key and relevant statistics for each country. The specific indicators vary between countries, as comparable data is not consistently recorded or available for all.

Crime Against Women by Region

Bangladesh


Gender-based Violence (GBV) against women in Bangladesh is often understood primarily through the lens of Intimate Partner Violence (IPV). Around 70% of women have experienced some form of IPV—ranging from physical abuse to economic control. In 2024 alone, 54% of women reported having been physically or sexually abused by their husbands at some point in their lives (UNFPA, 2025). While the following discussion may address GBV in other contexts, it is important to recognize how the prevalence of direct threats and assault shapes public perception. This narrow focus often contributes to a lack of awareness about more complex and systemic forms of female oppression .

India

Gender-based violence (GBV) against women in India is as multifaceted as its cultural and religious landscape. Sexual violence, including rape, remains a critical issue, with approximately 31,516 reported cases of rape, 6,450 cases of acid attacks, and 85,310 cases of kidnapping and abduction of women in 2022 alone (NCRB). However, it is important to note that these numbers do not fully capture India's situation, as many cases remain unreported; for instance, the National Crime Records Bureau estimated that in 2015, 85% of sexual violence cases went unreported, a number that has seen minimal decrease (NCRB, 2015). In addition to these, women face significant harassment and cyber violence, including online abuse, stalking, and the non-consensual sharing of intimate content, which are escalating at an alarming rate. These forms of violence are often overlooked or normalized within society, and compounded by limited awareness.


Pakistan




Gender-based violence (GBV) against women in Pakistan is closely tied to cultural norms, often justified under the pretense of religion, which reinforce rigid gender hierarchies. A woman’s role is framed within the confines of family honor, and any perceived transgression—whether in dress, mobility, or choice of partner—can invite violence. Beyond provincial statistics, approximately 24,000 cases of abduction were reported across Pakistan in 2024, often linked to rape, forced marriage, forced labor, or trafficking (Anees, 2025). Harassment in public spaces and online has also increased, encompassing stalking and the non-consensual sharing of intimate content. This normalization of violence is rooted not in a lack of awareness, but in a societal acceptance of such acts, where challenging the status quo is considered taboo and even speaking openly about these issues can carry severe social consequences.

Patriarchal Norms

South Asia operates within deeply patriarchal systems that are embedded in its economic structures, state policies, cultural norms, and religious misinterpretations so deeply rooted they are like air —unquestioned and ever-present. There is no system, structure, or relation, familial or corporate, that is not based on patriarchy (Nainer, 2023). Throughout South Asia’s history, from its pre-colonial to post-colonial modern era, every institution has reinforced a woman’s dependence on male family members. This results in each relationship defining her value in the roles she plays across different stages: daughter, sister, wife, and finally, mother (Nainer, 2023).

This economic and social marginalization, paired with social customs like paraya dhann (someone else’s wealth) and pardah (veiling), assigns the family’s moral responsibility to the women. As a woman’s social value is shaped through the inevitability of marriage and transfer of status from one family to another, daughters are often seen as “another mouth to feed, an economic liability rather than an asset (Niaz & Hassan, 2006).

The urgency that comes with getting rid of a daughter results in the ever-prevalent practice of dowry, a sum of money paid by the bride’s family as a transactional element to “support the newlywed couple”, and child marriage. Though both practices have been criminalized, they have not disappeared. Forgeries, such as that of a minor in Karnataka, India, whose Aadhaar card (identity card) was forged to increase her age by five years, create the illusion of legality. In other areas of society, notably Mumbai, dowry takes the form of opulence, as seen in the trays of custom-made sweets and luxury gifts from the bride’s family. Although the circumstances may differ — one driven by poverty, the other by a desire to ensure a seamless marriage process—both stem from a shared desperation to marry daughters off at a socially sanctioned moment.

These patrairchal views continue to thrive in digital spaces due to social media, as algorithms push for exceedingly misogynistic content from creators out for engagement and virality. This becomes evident especially when women enter traditionally male-dominated areas like gaming, tech forums, or political discourse, as they receive threats to intimidate them and preserve the status quo. [Example: Taniya Khanijow, a vlogger, receiving hate for commenting on the lack of safety for women] Algorithms then further reinforce harmful stereotypes through echo chambers, spaces limited to similar beliefs, used to reinforce confirmation bias, disguised as “user-preference” (Regher et al, 2024).

This inherently hostile online environment shapes how South Asian women navigate digital spaces.Many adopt strategies like self-censorship or choose to express themselves through private or anonymous accounts. While this masking can serve as a means of self-protection, it also reflects a broader cultural discomfort with women’s privacy and autonomy. In South Asian communities, autonomy — whether online or offline — is often met with harsh consequences, including institutional and interpersonal surveillance. Parents may demand unrestricted access to their daughters’ devices; spouses might monitor their online activities; and extended family members may treat a woman’s social media presence as a reflection of her morality (Swaddle, 2022).

As a result, many women turn to anonymity not just as a safeguard, but as a necessity for self-expression. Yet even this pursuit of privacy is met with suspicion and backlash. Privacy is framed not as a right, but a conditional privilege — its pursuit equated with dishonor, secrecy, or defiance. Men are socialized to value and exercise independence; women, by contrast, are conditioned to prioritize family cohesion and social conformity. This dynamic further entrenches patriarchal expectations around women’s behavior, both online and offline (The Swaddle, 2022)

The cost of visibility, therefore, is extremely high. One of the most violent manifestations of this is doxxing — the public release of a woman’s personal information, like her home address, phone number, or workplace. Although doxxing, an undeniable violation of privacy, in South Asian societies, it is often rationalized as a form of punishment — a way to “teach her a lesson” or to reassert control over a woman who is perceived to have stepped out of line. The underlying logic relies on “putting women in their place” and away from inhabiting male-dominated digital spaces. Therefore, if a woman’s online presence challenges expectations of modesty or conformity, her punishment must extend beyond the screen into her real, physical life.

Weak Legal Frameworks

The way a country treats women — both in law and in practice — provides a lens into its broader societal conditions and a nation’s commitment to serving justice in its pursuit of equality. This connection becomes especially evident in regions like South Asia, where the legal responses to gender-based violence (GBV) are often informed by patriarchal norms that tether a woman’s value to her perceived modesty, making women the bearers of familial honor. This notion of virtue is hence translated into legislation meant to protect women, but prioritizes the safeguard of “honor” rather than addressing the structural causes of violence (Nainar, 2023).

At the root of this issue lies the imbalance of women in legislative bodies across the region, as only 15-20% of women hold parliamentary seats in both upper and lower houses in India and Pakistan (UN Women,2023), and similarly, Bangladeshi women hold only 20.9% of political positions across all levels of government (UN Women Data Hub, 2024).

Women in Parliament

This absence of gender-inclusive governance can be blamed on the cultural expectations that associate leadership with masculinity and the disproportionate burden of domestic and caregiving responsibilities that restrict women from fully engaging in political life. Furthermore, female politicians are often subjected to heightened scrutiny over their appearance, behavior, and familial roles (The Swaddle, 2021). Hence, laws that were designed to protect women are frequently authored and interpreted through a male-centric lens, leaving even well-intentioned provisions to often insulate perpetrators and perpetuate systemic inequality, leaving these frameworks to remain largely ineffective.

The constitution’s reliance on vague, morality-laden language— especially in prioritizing a woman’s honor, modesty, and social standing over justice— has led to controversial interpretations. Such conditions create loopholes that fail to hold perpetrators accountable when a woman’s honor in the courtroom can be argued against in light of their age, career, social status, or caste (Naharisetty, 2022). A prominent example of this was seen in the State of Punjab v. Major Singh, AIR 1967 SC 63 (1966), where a 7.5-month-old infant was sexually assaulted, and the defence argued that the concept of “modesty” could not apply to the victim due to her age. This argument arose primarily due to the Indian Penal Code (IPC)§ 354 (2013), pre-amendment, which defined sexual assault as the intent “to outrage a woman’s modesty,” without defining 'modesty’.

Although India has seen some reform in its laws in response to public outrage over particularly gruesome sexual violence over centuries, many of these amendments appear to be protective; their language reinforces patriarchal norms by centering female “dignity” rather than autonomy. By protecting ‘modesty’ rather than autonomy or personal boundaries, IPC § 509 (2013) frames harm as a violation of social decorum rather than an infringement on a woman’s right to self-determination. Additionally, IPC § 228-A (1860) prohibits the disclosure of a rape victim’s identity, often suppressing the public reckoning necessary for systemic changes. These sections not only result in the dehumanization of victims and turn them into symbols of shame and sorrow, but also show their intent to hold the status quo and deter the uncertainty of actively working against the patriarchal society(Nema, 2025).

Pakistan’s Prevention of Electronic Crimes Act (PECA) (2016) presents a similar case of vagueness, like statements with an excessive emphasis on “morality”. It uses undefined ideologies to grant authorities the power to “remove or block access to information” if deemed necessary in the interest of “the glory of Islam,” national security, public order, or “decency or morality,” which leaves it to subjective interpretation. While this act theoretically provides mechanisms to combat cyber harassment, its enforcement is marred due to insufficient police training in digital literacy, and a deeper gap in understanding gender based digital violence. Consequently, victims are often silenced by shame, fear, or intimidation of social scrutiny in exchange for no tangible reparations (Naseer & Ashraf, 2021).

This incompetence in tackling gender-based violence is seen in the 2021 case of Marya Zulfiqar, where the police failed to offer her any protection or even summon the man she accused of threatening and holding her at gunpoint. Her subsequent murder underscores the dire consequences of a system that treats threats against women as secondary to ideological concerns or bureaucratic inaction. Although legal amendments have been seen in Pakistan, like the Protection of Women Criminal Laws Act (2006), nominally expanded protections to curb the misuse of zina (adultery) laws, it ultimately continued to place the burden of proof on victims. If a woman fails to prove rape, her accusation can itself be treated as prima facie evidence of adultery or fornication — offenses still punishable under the amended law (Lari, 2014).

In Bangladesh, the rise of DGBV — including deepfakes, impersonation, and identity theft — has led to the proposition of the Cyber Security Act § 39 (2023) that sentences 5 years in prison or a fine of 0.5 million Taka (approximately 4,000 USD), or both to a person that intentionally uses a computer network or device to assume another person’s identity or present their personal information with intent to deceive or defraud. However, due to the structural delays of legal processes, this vulnerability in the constitution leaves many victims without timely recourse, creating a dangerous gap in protection.

This vulnerability was made visible in 2023 when Bangladesh witnessed its first high-profile celebrity deepfake case of actress Tanjin Tisha in a sexually explicit video across several social media platforms. After online fact-checking and media research were done by an organization named Dismislab, the video was confirmed to be a fabrication, manipulated to superimpose Tanjin Tisha’s face onto footage from a pornographic website (Hossain, 2024). A similar case occurred involving actress Sabnam Faria, who publicly condemned the repeated misuse of her image in digitally altered pictures through a Facebook post on March 31st, 2024:

“For quite some time now, someone has been editing this person’s pictures and putting my face on them, and posting them on Facebook. I don’t know who these people are or what they want. Do you really have such a strong desire to see me in shorts? When I travel abroad, I already post photos wearing shorts! If those photos aren’t revealing enough and that disappoints you, then I apologize! But now one of your 'big brothers' has gone and edited her photo with my face again!!!”

Despite her strong public stance, the harassment persisted, and just a month later, on April 28th, she was forced to respond to yet another case of impersonation using an altered photo falsely attributed to her (Faria, 2024). These incidents underscore not only the increasing sophistication of digital GBV in Bangladesh but also the legal and institutional inertia that allows such violations to continue unchecked.

Revenge Porn


One of the most prevalent forms of Digital Gender-Based Violence is Revenge Porn, which is defined as ‘sexually explicit images of a person posted online without that person’s consent, especially as a form of revenge or harassment’ (Merriam-Webster Dictionary, 2015). By circulating sexually explicit images without consent, perpetrators not only seek to humiliate women but also reassert control over their bodies, reducing them to objects of public scrutiny. Hence, in the socially patriarchal environment of South Asia where a woman’s perceived “honor” is closely tied to her modesty and behavior, revenge porn in the form of creating fake profiles that share sexualized imagery under the guise of the victim’s identity, not only threaten her safety, but also her and her family’s “honor”/social identity.

This phenomenon is seen through crimes like:

  • The circulation of revenge porn of an ex-girlfriend in response to failed extortion by Bhubaneswar in India (Mohapatra, 2024)
  • The blackmailing of Sanam Maher by an ex-boyfriend in Pakistan (Yousafzai, 2022)
  • The circulation of intimate pictures by a fake ID, Shimanto Chowdhury, in Bangladesh

Each case showed the perpetrator’s invasion of privacy by an entrusted ex-romantic partner in an attempt to coerce the victim to comply with their demands or face becoming a social pariah.

However, the motivations behind revenge porn are not always rooted in failed social relationships; they can also emerge from broader patterns of social exclusion and humiliation, challenging the assumption that such crimes are strictly the byproduct of intimate relationship breakdowns. This can be seen by the first case of revenge porn in India in 2001, where a 16-year-old student was charged for making a pornographic website of his classmates and teacher, for socially ostracizing and mocking him. While clearly a form of digital violence, the legal response was notably lenient as the court classified the act as “tech-graffiti,” likening it to offensive messages written in public restrooms, citing it to be a concern for his future and well-being (CHAKRAVART, 2001).

Social Stigma & Victim Blaming

Izzat, which directly translates to honor or respect, is a deeply rooted cultural concept in South Asia that applies not only to individuals but also to the reputation of entire families. Women, in particular, are positioned as the custodians of family and national honor [Example: Putting the onus of national honor on bollywood actresses] through the expectation of dignity, chastity, and compulsory virtue. This linkage is reinforced through cultural norms, religious interpretations, and social practices that impose strict codes of conduct for women, making their bodies and decisions sites of collective control and surveillance. As a result, women’s bodies and choices become sites of control and surveillance in the name of protecting family or community prestige (Ali, 2025).

This regulation of women can be broadly categorized into two interrelated forms: familial regulation and self-regulation (Aksar et al., 2020). Familial regulation refers to the explicit restrictions imposed by family members, limiting a woman’s mobility, social interactions, and participation in public and digital spaces. In response, women frequently internalize these expectations, resulting in consciously tailored online and offline behavior to align with family expectations.

This policing can be seen in a study that explored how Pakistani women navigate social media within their patriarchal society and how their online experiences are heavily shaped by cultural concepts like parda (veiling). This is not limited to lower-income families, as even uneducated women use self-censorship to protect family reputation, when the simple act of posting pictures in jeans needs to be done through a fake profile (Aksar et al., 2020). Similarly, Indian Muslim women also curated their online presence to align with familial expectations of decency, modesty, and decorum (Mishara & Basu, 2014).

In extreme cases, violations of these norms are met with honor-based violence, such as karo kari or “honor killings”. These acts involve being murdered by their family members for behaviors perceived as dishonorable, such as refusing arranged marriages, expressing autonomy, or embracing non-traditional lifestyles. A stark example is the case of Qandeel Baloch, a Pakistani social media personality whose bold online presence and fashion challenged the role of women in Pakistani society. This refusal of the status quo resulted in her court summons in June 2016 by the Baloch ethnic group, who accused her of bringing shame to their people by using the Baloch surname for her internet persona(Saifi et al., 2022).

Soon after, her anonymity was stripped away as her personal information was leaked by a journalist, which put her at risk of recognition, social persecution, and caused her to feel unsafe within her own home. On July 12, 2016, Qandeel Baloch was found dead, murdered by the hands of her brother in the name of family honor. While initially charged and imprisoned, Waseem was pardoned in 2022 by their mother under Pakistan’s Penal Code § 311, which permits a victim’s family to forgive the assailant—a legal loophole often exploited in honor killing cases (Saifi et al., 2022).

Despite the severity of this societal norm, religious communities advocate these acts as “domestic issues”, reflecting a tolerance or even justification of violence against women in the name of tradition. However, Ali & Zafar (2020) assert that these crimes are driven by cultural customs, and not Islam, which is the most widely practiced religion in Pakistan. They further highlight that the societal reverence for assailants is shaped not only by pressure to preserve honor but also by an admiration for their “bravery” and adherence to communal and religious values over kin.

This admiration of positive traits (eg, bravery, “religious adherence”, etc.) influences the perception of unrelated traits like moral integrity. In the context of honor killings, perpetrators are glorified, not because murder is condoned, but because the violence took place to uphold religious norms. This phenomenon worsens in rural and conservative areas of South Asia, as limited access to education and economic dependence leave women with little knowledge of their rights (Nema, 2023). As weak law enforcement infrastructure and cultural complicity among local leaders provide perpetrators with near-impunity, South Asian governments trap their women in a cycle of gender-based violence and communal validation solely at the expense of their lives.

As for comparatively “trivial” consequences, victims may face societal restrictions and are often blamed for the crime and its consequences due to their actions that either provoke men (i.e, being outspoken, dressed immodestly, and walking without a male chaperone), or fail to resist the assailant (Rawat, 2017). In some egregious cases, survivors are not only blamed but also forced to marry their rapists to “restore family honor” and avoid any social burdens that may come from pressing charges. In Bangladesh, at least 20 rape survivors were reportedly married off to their accused rapists under the pressure of village elders, police, or family members (Karim, 2025). Hence, it is not uncommon for women faced with abuse or assault to refrain from reporting incidents and withdraw from society or digital platforms in some form (Digital Rights Foundation, 2020; Internet Democracy Project, 2019)

Another form of failure from the constant vilification can be seen in the legal system of women in cases of everyday offline gender-based harassment. In one such incident, a female student at Dhaka University faced violent death and rape threats after actively taking part in the July movement in Bangladesh (Nilima, 2025). She stated:

“This relentless attack has left me traumatised. The way I have been made recognizable is intended to get me killed.”

The legal inertia that comes from systems refusing to provide women justice worsens in light of digital platforms, as victims are pushed to remove themselves from masculine spaces of politics and social commentary. This was seen in Bangladesh, where another student, Farzana Sithi, faced over 250 fake accounts spreading lies about her for taking part in the July movement in Bangladesh, has been forced into hiding due to physical threats and false cases against her. (Nilima, 2025). She stated:

“I did not receive any assistance from law enforcement. Some suggested private firms could disable fake accounts, but their services cost {money} dollars -- I can't afford that. I still use public transport. And is this even my responsibility?”

This creates a vicious cycle fueled by complacency in social and online settings, where women are emotionally burdened while factors like upbringing, socialization, and cultural context go unchallenged. (Chinchu, 2024; Bessant & Watts, 2021). Although this action may contribute to less repeated exposure and secondary victimization, the burden of the crime is placed on the victim rather than the perpetrator (Tonny & Nawaz, 2025; Naseet & Ashrad, 2022). These expectations are then followed by:

What were you doing there?
Why would you speak out?
Why are you even on social media?

In these circumstances, the victim no longer has control of the narrative, which causes extreme psychological distress on the victim via societal alienation and a deep-rooted guilt. This can result in psychological distress, including signs of anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), all contributing to the struggle of self-blame and a disrupted self-concept clarity (Ali, 2025; Halder & Jaishankar, 2013). A study followed 100 sexually abused adolescent girls over six months to understand how self-blame , shame, and avoidance coping affected their mental health. Results showed that girls who felt deep shame initially were more likely to develop PTSD symptoms later, while self-blame predicted higher depression levels. Avoidance coping (like suppressing emotions) and ongoing depression both strongly contributed to suicidal thoughts, explaining over half of these cases. Six months later, nearly half the girls were reported to have PTSD symptoms, over a third faced depression, and 30% reported suicidal thoughts (Alix et al., 2020).

The asymmetry between the actions of the perpetrator and the consequences faced by the victim reflects how communities that uphold izzat disavow survivors of Gender-Based Violence. Through silence and stigma, moral blame is redirected onto the victim, reinforcing a cycle of shame and marginalization.

Honor is communal; shame is individual.

Men’s Rights & Anti‐Feminist Movements

The culture of silence and blame is further weaponized in digital spaces. In India, the rhetoric of the Men’s Rights Movement (MRM) reframes victims of sexual assault as manipulative and perpetrators as victims of a “biased system. They often refer to several sections of the Indian constitution as “made for the protection of women” despite the inherently patriarchal structure of Indian society. These criticisms of the law and its enforcers are especially magnified in cases of custody, alimony, divorce, and domestic violence (Desai, 2023). What began as a purported defense of men’s legal rights has increasingly devolved into a justification for misogyny, the dismissal of gender-based violence, and the vilification of feminism.

The evolution of Web 2.0 – characterized by the exponential growth of accessibility, users, and interactive platforms like Reddit, YouTube, and Instagram – enabled open public communication, allowing MRM ideologies to take root in anonymous online spaces (Desai, 2023). While the manosphere rhetoric originated in the West, in South Asia, particularly India, it is shaped by socio-religious conservatism and linguistic diversity. A notable concept they employ is the idea of the Adarsh Bhartiya Nari, which translates to the Ideal Indian Woman, a submissive female archetype that devoutly embodies Indian values, thereby framing feminism as a threat to national culture and morality (Priya & Sigamany, 2025).

Online misogyny in these regions often manifests in two forms: explicit and implicit. Explicit misogyny can be classified as personal insults to the victim using sexually derogatory targeted speech on the victim’s moral and intellectual worth based on her gender (Waseem et. al, 2017). A stark example of this phenomenon is the online harassment Rhea Chakraborty faced following Sushant Singh Rajput’s 2020, her then partner’s suicide. In a study examining 2,128 posts with relevant hashtags to the case, the majority of the X posts were found to have misogynistic remarks towards Chakraborty. She was accused of being intellectually inferior, morally corrupt, a “gold-digger,” a witch, and Sushant’s murderer despite a lack of evidence (Nanditha et al., 2023).

Similarly, in Bangladesh, during the 2015 Pohela Boishakh celebrations, a group of men assaulted around 20 women in Dhaka, stripped and filmed them in an abhorrent manner. As videos of the attack spread online, many used religious justifications to blame the victims for being alone and implying that they were asking for it. After the victims and their supporters, the majority of whom were women, publicly demanded justice, they were met with further abuse and threats of sexual violence on social media and blogs (Akter, 2024). This event demonstrated how real-world hostility can fuel online violence and collective aggression.

On the other hand, implicit misogyny operates through sarcasm, cynicism, and subtle accusations (Waseem et al., 2017). Influencer Apoorva Mukhija, who had a viral altercation on a talent show called “India’s Got Latent,” faced a major one-way backlash, including threats of acid attack, rape, and death. On April 8, 2025, in a post where she shares uncensored comments she received after the incident, her caption read– “and that’s not even 1%.”

Although her fans showed her a lot of support and positivity, there were a noticeable number of comments that resorted to sarcasm, speculation, and insinuation. Several comments on her post imply that she only revealed the threats to gain sympathy and downplay the abhorrent nature of the backlash she received initially– or deserved the actions behind the threats. Dehumanizing and misogynistic jokes are often cloaked under “dark humor” to agitate female users by dismissing their struggles or calls to equality. They serve as a dog whistle to allow people from similar ideological backgrounds to amplify digital spaces to channel their misogynistic ideologies (Cunha, 2020).

This phenomenon extends beyond casual misogyny aimed at individual women on platforms to women as a whole, using whataboutism, a rhetorical strategy in which legitimate concerns are dismissed by deflecting to unrelated or exaggerated counterclaims. In the context of online gender discourse, this takes the form of citing false domestic abuse, rape, or dowry cases against innocent men to undermine movements like #MeToo and victims. While it is important to acknowledge that false accusations do occur, whataboutism shifts the possibility of criticizing the pitfalls of the legislature to foster public distrust in all victims.

This inherent distrust in the victim and the system has resulted in the rape myth propaganda, which blames female autonomy and situational context to justify the assailant. In male-dominated online spaces, such views manifest themselves through oft-repeated and unverified statistics like “75% of rape cases are false” and “80% of domestic violence and dowry cases in India found to be false” without credible sources. The deliberate exaggeration and weaponization of misinformation by red-pill influencers cultivates mistrust toward women and dilutes public empathy toward victims. By selectively amplifying the occurrence of false accusations, these people fuel a broader ecosystem of doubt and misogyny. The aim is not factual and informed discourse, but rather disregards structural gender-based violence and reframes male discomfort with shifting gender norms as victimhood.

Film

Bangladeshi cinema

Despite the modern female-centric rhetoric of Bangladeshi cinema in films like Under Construction or classics like Golapi Ekhon Traine, a majority of mainstream content sidelines women to caregivers, victims, or points of objectification. Following societal norms, they are defined by their relationships with men as lovers, mothers, or maids. The pervasive “damsel in distress” trope frames them as weak, dependent, and targets of villains to hurt the male leads.

Bangladeshi cinema has a troubling pattern of using violence against women, especially kidnapping and rape, as a tool to hurt or challenge male heroes. The woman’s body becomes a battleground for male ego, with her trauma framed only as a motivation for the hero's revenge rather than as an act of violence against her autonomy.

Films like Valobashlei Ghor Bandha Jay Na, Amar Jaan Amar Praan, and Poramon 2 showcase female leads subjected to physical abuse–either by family for defying norms or by antagonists seeking revenge. The troubling “dumb woman” trope further dehumanized women, portraying them as unintelligent and deserving of mistreatment. In the 2006 hit Chacchu, for example, the hero who was a police officer was chasing an alleged kidnapper. He gets stopped mid chase by his girlfriend who says, “Cruel lover, you are attentive about the culprits, but not the heart of your girlfriend” followed by a slap from the hero who blames her for the culprit escaping. This instance of intimate partner violence is justified in the story because her character is portrayed as ‘dumb’ who doesn’t understand the importance of his work.

Indian cinema

Cinema in the Indian subcontinent holds deep cultural significance, particularly for the middle class, influencing values, identity, and gender norms. Despite the narrative progress, mainstream films have long been a burden on women to either gratify men through preserving purity or sexualize themselves in the name of empowerment.

The prior ideology reveals itself in cinema through submissive women or “damsels in distress” as the story is told through the lens of a young and often patriotic/family-oriented man. Films like Kabir Singh (2019) and Animal (2023), both directed by Sandeep Reddy Vanga, show consistent patterns of patriarchal storytelling. These box office hits glorified toxic masculinity by justifying violence, emotional abuse, and infidelity as expressions of male trauma and, worst of all, male nature (Khan & Taylor, 2023).

This gendered framing is not new. The idea of the Adarsh Bhartiya Nari (Ideal Indian Woman) has long dominated Bollywood, as seen in the iconic film ‘Dil Wale Dulhaniya Le Jayenge’ (1995). The female lead, Simran, is depicted as the embodiment of purity and cultural virtue–her character symbolically carrying the burden of national honor. In one notable scene, after a night of drinking, she wakes up in the male lead’s shirt and panics, fearing her body, and by extension, her honor, had been compromised. However, the protagonist, Raj, reassures her by saying he would never take advantage of her because he understands the importance of an Indian woman’s honor. While noble at face value, the scene reinforces the notion that a woman’s worth is tied to her chastity, and it is ultimately the man who is positioned as her moral guardian.

Although the second trope women often fall into, that of hypersexuality, manifests in various ways, it is most prominently seen in the portrayals of women in “item songs”. They frequently present female bodies as sexual spectacles, either through the way the camera glides over her body or the often faceless crowd of men she aims to please. While some may interpret a woman singing and dancing openly about her sexuality as empowering, it is important to consider the broader context behind the intended audience and the environment it takes place in (Shewade, 2023).

Pakistani cinema

Since the inception of Pakistan’s first national television network in 1964, soap operas have held a strong appeal among female audiences, largely due to their reflection of the everyday lives and struggles of women in the country. However, over the years, these dramas have become synonymous with problematic portrayals of gender dynamics. Repetitive narratives that trivialize domestic abuse, pit women against each other, demonize them in public spheres, and frame them as ‘evil’ or ‘promiscuous’ have earned this popular medium a long-standing reputation for promoting regressive values under the guise of tradition (Khan, 2020).

In Ahmar’s, a Pakistani journalist and director of the media advocacy organization Uks’s words, “99.9% of dramas on Pakistani television channels are ‘sexist, repressive, and regressive”(Sarfaraz, 2020). The central theme of these serial dramas has always ranged from honour, Islamic notions of piety, chador and chaar deewari, or the traditionalist concept of veil and the four walls of a house being synonymous with societal protection, to motherhood and the notions of the good/bad woman, etc (Khan, 2020).

One particularly harmful and recurring trope is that of the dominant female character depicted as materialistic and morally corrupt, pressuring her husband to engage in illegal activities such as smuggling or bribery to satisfy her desire for luxury items like gold, designer clothing, or lavish homes. This narrative, seen in dramas such as Mera Saaein (2010), shifts the blame for male corruption onto women, absolving men of accountability and reinforcing patriarchal structures (Dutoya, 2018, p. 291).

Women in these dramas are often confined to domestic spaces, portrayed as obedient daughters, wives, or mothers upholding patriarchal and cultural values. Career women, in contrast, are typically characterized as manipulative, emotionally cold, or morally deviant (Fatima, 2019; Ashfaq & Shafiq, 2018; Abbas, 2018; Ullah & Nisar, 2011). Men, meanwhile, are granted the public domain—depicted as rational, ambitious, and morally grounded figures (Abbas, 2018; Ullah & Nisar, 2011).

Female characters who challenge these norms are often punished—narratively and socially—by male authority figures such as husbands, fathers, or brothers. A 2009 study on the portrayal of violence in Pakistani dramas found that most on-screen psychological violence is committed by male relatives within domestic spaces (Pervez et al., 2009; 2010). Although female antagonists appear less frequently, they are often cast as domineering in-laws or senior figures who lack compassion, particularly if they hold a higher economic or social status. At the same time, a woman’s desirability is measured by her conformity to idealized standards of femininity and subservience (Khan, 2020).

The 2018 drama Zun Mureed provides a stark example of how domestic violence is trivialized and victims are shamed. When the main character Tabassum calls the police after her husband Sajjad assaults her, the legal response—though within her rights—is met with harsh judgment and social ostracization. The show frames male aggression as a "bimari" or sickness, implying it is beyond the abuser’s control and thus absolving him of responsibility.

Zun Mureed also features a number of overtly misogynistic dialogues. In one instance, a male character laments, “Times have changed… now even women’s lies are being heard… gone are the days when no one paid heed to a woman’s truth” (episode 25), equating women’s voices with deceit. Another scene trivializes abuse with the line, “Pious women die at the hands of their husbands, but never utter a single word—and you called the police for a mere scratch” (episode 5), reinforcing silence and endurance as markers of female virtue. Earlier in the series, a servant character states, “I am a servant, not anyone’s wife… I can only tolerate such behavior from my husband” (episode 3), implying that even domestic workers command more respect than wives.

Blogger Daima Hussein captured the deep cultural harm of such narratives in her critique of the hit drama Humsafar, noting:

“These kinds of dramas should not be shown, as we already live in a society where women always have to prove themselves or suffer the wrath of their families—fathers, brothers, and husbands. This seems very unfair” (Hussein, 2022).

She highlights how the male lead, Asher, blindly trusts his mother over his pregnant wife, Khirad, without evidence, mirroring real-world dynamics of mistrust and control over women within family structures.

Influencer culture

Harmful gender norms are not only cultivated through formal media, but are increasingly amplified by influencer culture, repackaging misogyny as masculinity and selling it to millions.

Indian red pill influencers, many inspired by Western figures like Andrew Tate and Jordan Peterson, use South Asia’s linguistic diversity to shape how they spread misogyny online. Slang terms like feminichi (a blend of “feminist” and “chi”, a common exclamation of disgust in India) are used to mock feminist advocacy by women. Similarly, pavada (skirt, a feminising insult) and “peacock feminism” –often synonymous with performative feminism– are used to mock and delegitimise male feminist allies (Chinchu, 2025)

Many influencers mask misogyny under the guise of “self-improvement” or “wellness” content. Figures like Sarthak Goel and Elvish Yadav portray women as obstacles to male success, promoting emotional detachment and dominance. Goel’s series, “100 Days of Breaking a Girl’s Ego,” teaches manipulation as a virtue and discourages empathy. These creators often attribute men’s romantic failures to “female hypergamy” and cultural Westernization, reinforcing toxic worldviews while deflecting blame from systemic or emotional complexities.

This influencer pipeline thrives on parasocial dynamics–one-sided emotional bonds between creators and viewers–where advice is consumed as gospel. The influencer becomes a digital guru, profiting not only from attention but also through monetized “solutions”. For example, Jaskaran Singh (@jasexplains on Instagram) markets The Art of Chatting Organic, a ₹89 dating course (1.02 dollars), to vulnerable young men. The low price ensures mass appeal, while the content teaches that women are irrational puzzles to be solved, further fueling objectification.

In Pakistan, similar influencer models appear in the form of “mincels” or “Muslim incels,” such as Dawah Bros, who cloak misogyny in religious purity. These online ecosystems foster digital radicalization, where disillusioned young men are groomed into communities that glorify patriarchal domination, emotional suppression, and gendered hate. Researchers argue that this culture is not new but rather a monetized extension of long-standing patriarchal norms. Influencers benefit from virality by creating "rage bait" content, sometimes imitating figures like Andrew Tate, whose online clubs promote controversial and exploitative ideologies. This shift has led some previously neutral men to shift to more extreme views, isolating women and enforcing rigid gender roles under the guise of Islam.

This phenomenon has contributed to a brand of “online Islam” operated by unqualified influencers who mimic right-wing, often Islamophobic, ideologies while attacking feminism. Journalist Yousra Samir Imran, once active in confronting this culture online, now distances herself due to the emotional toll. Instead, she challenges harmful beliefs through her writing. She notes how these influencers ignore Islamic values of compassion and equality, misusing weak or misrepresented religious sources to justify their misogyny. Women are often the main targets, though even dissenting men face online abuse and exclusion from the community.

Digital Feminism

Feminism, despite modern reinterpretations, continues to hold profound significance globally–especially in South Asia– despite the strong cultural diversity of the subcontinent. Historically, feminist identification has allowed survivors to contextualize abuse, whether it be emotional or physical, as a product of gendered oppression. It helps them externalize their blame and move away from self-perceived personal failure (Dey, 2019).

However, these conversations were limited to academic and upper-class circles that excluded women based on lower castes and economic standing, like Dalits, Bahujan, and Adivasi women. Despite the inherent empowering values past feminist communities presented, it presented rape through the lens of a modest, family-oriented, and submissive woman from the middle or upper class — creating the ideology of an “ideal rape” or an “ideal victim,”.This makes it so that claims or individuals that do not fit the narrative are devalued by the predetermined ideology used to “measure and judge one’s experience of rape” (Dey, 2019).

In current dynamics, digital and collective feminist identification has proven to bring different perspectives and be a considerably greater tool in tackling difficult conversations in each country.

Bangladesh


In Bangladesh, digital spaces have seen the emergence of digital movements like #Justice for Tonu, demanding accountability for the 2016 rape and murder of Sohagi Jahan Tonu, a university student and theatre activist found dead inside a military cantonment. Despite the sensitive nature of the location and institutional resistance to transparency, public pressure sustained the campaign for years and challenged one of the most rigid of power structures. Feminist Facebook groups like Nari: Mohila Bus Service Limited, while initially aimed at creating safer transportation, became vital platforms where women could share experiences of harassment and voice frustrations over systemic inaction. This digital intimacy, often lacking in public or institutional spaces, provides emotional relief and fosters mutual care. More than that, it offers a cognitive reframing to begin to understand how entrenched norms, institutional apathy, and cultural narratives construct and perpetuate their marginalization. As a result, the targets of shame begin to shift–from victims to perpetrators, from survivors to complicit institutions.

Across these three countries, digital feminism has facilitated what scholars refer to as “affective publics”–networks of people connected not only by shared information but by shared emotions and values (Papcharissi, 2025). These public spaces of political intimacy and radical empathy, where collective storytelling becomes both a mode of healing and a form of protest. Importantly, these feminist digital communities often recognize the intersectional nature of harm, amplifying the voices of Dalit, queer, rural, and working-class women who are frequently marginalised in mainstream narratives. In doing so, they not only challenge dominant paradigms but actively construct alternative feminist futures rooted in accountability, inclusivity, and care. This, collective activism, and identification within digital feminism do far more than raise awareness–they reconfigure power, redistribute shame, and create a politics of visibility and voice where none previously existed.

India

“Rape is not something that occurs by itself. Let’s ask ourselves how we, our society, we as people, create and sustain the mindset that leads to rape, how we make our men so violent, how we insult our women so regularly.” - Urvashi Bhutalia

Although feminism in India works to tackle several issues at hand, one of its biggest concerns is the dehumanization through verbal, physical, and digital sexual violations of women and children. In the past, organizations have focused on the sexual exploitation of women through rape cases and unfair laws.

The 2012 rape of Nirbhaya (meaning brave one) [more information] was a turning point. The brutality of the assault, where her ovaries were eviscerated with a metal rod, shocked the nation and the world. It sparked massive protests and media coverage, not only because of the inhumanity of the act but also because it exposed the deep-rooted crisis of sexual violence in India (BBC News, 2020). During this time, social media saw the hashtags #Nirbhaya, #JusticeForNirbhaya, #delhirapecase, and many more used to convey outrage, directed at not only the men but also law enforcement, legal systems, and the broader patriarchal society. Furthermore, the normalization of victim-blaming was emphasized by the comments made by the perpetrator and their lawyer. Some being:

"You can't clap with one hand, it takes two hands."

"We have the best culture. In our (Indian) culture, there is no place for a woman,"

"The girl or woman is more precious than a gem, than a diamond. It is up to you how you want to keep that diamond, if you put the diamond on the street, certainly the dog will take it. You can't stop him."

Building on this momentum, the 2018 #MeToo wave was catalyzed by Priya Ramani’s testimony against M.J. Akbar on acts of sexual abuse [more information on her legal struggles and victory], which exposed individual cases of harassment and allowed women, especially from urban and professional spheres (Mitra et al., 2021) . Women that participated in this movement finally reclaimed the narrative and validated their pain, often for the first time. Platforms like Youth Ki Awaz (The Voice of the Young) and Got Stared At rose to popularity and focused on challenging the cultural narratives around modesty and its weaponization to justify or downplay harassment. Although this agency of women disrupted primarily online spheres, they provide victims of all genders to seek refuge from society and shift the conversation to tackle the innate patriarchal nature of society, sometimes accompanied by directly posting pictures and videos of men actively harassing women on social media.

Pakistan

“I raise up my voice–not so I can shout, but so that those without a voice can be heard.” - Malala Yousafzai

In Pakistan, a similar exponential rise of feminist activism in digital spaces cannot be separated from the high-profile tragedies that materialize the country’s ideals around patriarchal norms in a way that hurts women as no other society does. This indifference to women’s safety was seen in the 2018 rape and murder of 7-year-old Zainab Ansari in Kasur, whose body was found by her family members after 5 days of searching due to the police’s neglect of the matter. The public outrage following these incidents directly influences legislative change, culminating in the passing of the Anti-Rape Ordinance of 2020, also known as "Zainab's Law." This landmark ordinance sought to address systemic failings to Pakistan's response to sexual violence through the creation of special courts to expedite rape trials within four months, the establishment of sex offender registry to track convicted offenders, and the possibility of chemical castration as a punishment for repeat offenders

The digital feminist momentum that emerged from #JusticeforZainab and #StopVictimBlaming thus continued to push beyond legislation, demanding systemic change that addressed the deeper patriarchal structures enabling gender-based violence

Additionally, 2018 saw the Aurat March (Women’s March) took the initiative of holding physical demonstrations and disseminating feminist ideals online by providing counter-narratives to dominant cultural tropes that blame women for their own victimizations. Slogans like “Mera Jism, Meri Marzi” (My Body, My Choice) and banners confronting everyday misogyny became rallying cries for bodily autonomy and dignity. These sentiments were reignited in light of the 2020 motorway gang rape case, where a Pakistani-French woman stranded with her children was attacked on a major highway outside Lahore in front of her children. Although details of the crime itself were horrific, it was the victim-blaming that came from a senior police official in Lahore, who blamed the victim for traveling alone at night with her two children. Hashtags like #StopVicitmBlaming and #HangRapists were seen online as the conversation surrounded the full-throated rejection of the institutionalized misogyny that governs both public safety and societal judgment.

Despite the call to action by activists and citizens alike over structures to prevent crimes against women, the 2024 murder of the 17-year-old TikTok star Sana Yousaf underscores Gender Based Violence (GBV) underscores keeping an eye on digital misogyny, as it is not only confined to screens. Sana Yousaf and her father were shot on June 2nd, 2025, by Hayat as a result of her rejecting his advances and blocking him on social media. Her death became emblematic of how patriarchal entitlement adapts itself to the digital age. It is no longer applicable to keeping our women “safe” by restricting them, but rather teaching men to see women not as objects to control, pursue, or desire, but as human beings deserving of autonomy, respect, and space. At the end of the day–she was at home.

Our Mission
